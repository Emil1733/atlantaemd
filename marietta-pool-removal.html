Cobb County Soil Mechanics & Compaction Physics

In Marietta and across Cobb County, the primary geotechnical challenge is managing the characteristics of Georgia Red Clay (specifically Cecil clay loam) and weathered saprolite soils. Unlike sandy soils that drain naturally and compact easily under gravity, Cecil clay is highly cohesive, meaning it retains water and expands or contracts significantly depending on moisture content. When a swimming pool shell is removed, a large void is left behind. If this void is filled with uncompacted soil, the clay will absorb water during Atlanta's heavy spring rains, swelling in volume, and then shrink during the dry summer months. This constant expansion-contraction cycle leads to severe ground settling (subsidence), resulting in a backyard full of uneven sinkholes and low spots where water pools.

To prevent this, our engineering-first protocol employs the Standard Proctor Compaction Test (ASTM D698). We test the soil to establish its Optimum Moisture Content (OMC)—the precise moisture level at which the soil reaches its maximum dry density. During the backfilling process, we import clean, select structural fill (free of organic debris, large rocks, or roots) and place it in thin, horizontal layers called "lifts," measuring no more than 6 to 8 inches in loose depth. Each lift is mechanically compacted using a combination of vibratory plates and walk-behind sheepsfoot rollers, which apply high-frequency impact force to knead the clay particles together and expel air pockets. We target a minimum of 95% Modified Proctor density. This ensures the filled area matches the load-bearing capacity of the surrounding virgin ground, making the land structurally stable for future landscaping, patios, or residential construction.

Hydrogeological Safety & The "Bathtub Effect" Mitigation

A common error made by general landscaping or demolition contractors is failing to address the hydrological reality of the pool shell. When performing a "partial pool abandonment"—where only the top few feet of the pool walls are demolished and the remaining pool bottom is left in place—the remaining concrete acts as an impermeable basin. In clay soils, rainwater that seeps down through the backfilled soil cannot penetrate the concrete shell. It becomes trapped, creating a subterranean reservoir. This phenomenon, known as the "Bathtub Effect," saturates the backfilled soil, destroying its structural integrity and turning your yard into a muddy, unstable marsh. The stagnant water trapped below the surface can also migrate laterally, damaging neighboring retaining walls and house foundations.

Our Marietta protocol mitigates this risk through precise hydrogeological fracturing. We use 5-ton hydraulic impact hammers to puncture the bottom of the gunite pool floor, drilling 18-inch diameter drainage ports every 4 feet across the entire basin. This ensures that groundwater and surface runoff can pass freely through the pool basin into the natural water table. Additionally, we place a 12-to-18-inch layer of washed #57 stone (crushed granite) at the very bottom of the pool shell. This acts as a subterranean drainage reservoir, directing water away from the compacted soil and preventing saturation. This attention to detail is why our site restorations pass Cobb County building inspections on the first attempt, protecting both your property and your peace of mind.

Surgical Access Engineering & Turf Preservation

Demolishing a pool requires heavy machinery, including compact track loaders, mini-excavators, and tandem-axle dump trucks. Transporting these multi-ton loads across a residential yard can cause significant damage, cracking driveways, crushing subterranean utility lines (such as irrigation systems, water service mains, or septic fields), and compacting the topsoil, which destroys the roots of specimen trees. In historic Marietta neighborhoods with mature oak and maple trees, protecting root systems is critical. Compacting the soil within a tree's Critical Root Zone (CRZ) starves the roots of oxygen and water, causing the tree to decline and die over several years.

To avoid this, we develop a detailed Ingress and Logistical Plan for every project. We lay down high-density polyethylene (HDPE) ground protection mats to form a temporary roadway. These mats distribute the weight of our machinery over a wide surface area, reducing ground pressure to less than 4 pounds per square inch (PSI)—less than the footprint of an average adult. For tight-access properties where gates or side setbacks are narrow (under 5 feet), we deploy specialized mini-loaders with a 36-inch width profile that can slide through standard garden gates, eliminating the need to tear down fence panels. This level of logistical care ensures that the only thing we leave behind is a beautiful, restored yard.

Stormwater Runoff & Hydrological Design in Marietta

Removing a swimming pool permanently alters the surface hydrology of your lot. An average in-ground swimming pool represents a large, impermeable surface that catches water and redirects it through plumbing or overflow channels. Replacing this structure with soil turns the area into a permeable surface that absorbs water, but also changes how stormwater flows across the yard. In sloped Marietta neighborhoods—especially those near Kennesaw Mountain or along the Chattahoochee River corridor—poorly managed surface runoff can erode new turf, wash away landscaping, or flood neighboring properties, creating major legal liabilities under Georgia's strict civil water laws.

To address this, our team integrates hydrological engineering into our final grading plans. We design and construct custom surface swales—shallow, grassy channels with gentle side slopes—that redirect surface runoff away from your house foundation and guide it toward approved municipal drainage points or designated rain gardens. On properties with high slopes, we install French drains containing perforated PVC pipe encased in aggregate filter sleeves and washed stone to collect and redirect subsurface water. By managing both surface grading and subsurface flow, we ensure that your reclaimed land stays dry and structurally sound, preventing erosion and keeping your yard perfectly usable even after the heaviest Georgia downpours.

How do you protect Marietta's historic driveways during demolition?

Older Marietta driveways often lack the thickness of modern concrete. We utilize low-ground-pressure (LGP) tracks and structural bridging (steel plates or HDPE mats) to ensure the 20-ton load of an excavator is distributed across a massive surface area, preventing cracks or substrate failure.

Yes, but only if you choose our Engineered Full Removal. We remove the entire shell and compact the backfill to 95% Modified Proctor density. We provide the certified testing data required to secure a building permit for an ADU, guest house, or permanent pavilion.

What happens to the existing Marietta pool plumbing?
